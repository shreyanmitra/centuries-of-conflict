Echoes of Battle

Which Wars History Chose to Remember — and Whose It Forgot

Two datasets. Thousands of recorded battles. One question: is the historical record of human warfare a mirror of reality — or a reflection of who held the pen?

Key Details
  • Not all battles are adequately recorded
  • Who decides which battles enter the historical canon?
  • Introducing the two datasets: Wikidata (crowdsourced encyclopedic) vs CDB90 (curated analytical dataset of ~1600–1982)
  • These datasets don't measure how many battles happened — they measure how many battles Western record-keepers thought were worth recording
On the twenty-second of January, 1879, a Zulu army annihilated a British imperial force at Isandlwana – the worst defeat the British Empire experienced in the nineteenth century from an indigenous people, and one of the most important battles in the colonial era. It appears in CDB90 exactly once, with a single sparse row: location, year, and approximate aggregate counts. There is no weather code, no terrain data, no Zulu commander, no breakdown of strength or casualties by belligerent. The Rapido River crossings of January 1944, on the other hand, fill several rows – one per day of the battle – with weather, front width, commanders on both sides, casualties computed to the nearest hundred. They are both real battles, one was studied, the other was noticed, ignored, and put aside. This project inhabits the intersection of two data sets: Wikidata, the open knowledge base behind Wikipedia, a democratic, imperfect, constantly evolving collection of everything, including all battles, that someone, anywhere, somehow, thought important enough to encode as data; and CDB90, a 1991 U.S. Army dataset of 660 battles selected by military analysts for quantitative study of combat outcomes. Together they cover thousands of engagements across three millennia. Apart, they reveal something more interesting than any individual battle — a portrait of which wars Western record-keepers thought were worth remembering, and whose they forgot.
Section 01 — The Concentration

Two wars swallow nearly half the record

In the CDB90 dataset — 660 battles spanning four centuries — World War I and World War II alone account for 48% of all entries. Every other conflict across 400 years shares the remainder. Click any square or ribbon to explore that war's individual battles.

Key Takeaways
  • CBD90 is a 1991 social science dataset of "important" battles selected by military analysts for quantitative study of combat outcomes
  • The selection bias is built in: these are battles analysts considered analytically significant — which skews toward documented, Western, state-on-state warfare
  • The dominance of WWI/WWII reflects two things: (1) those wars genuinely produced enormous numbers of discrete engagements; (2) they are the most thoroughly documented conflicts in Western military history
  • Note the near-absence of colonial conflicts, civil wars outside the U.S., and non-European warfare pre-1900
  • Toggle between Treemap and Stream Graph to see war intensity across time. Click any war to drill into its individual battles.

Section 03 — The Record

History is a story told with an accelerating pen

The number of documented battles explodes in the modern era. But did humans fight more often — or did we simply get better at writing things down? And who, exactly, was doing the writing?

Key Takeaways
  • Ancient empires fought thousands of battles, yet our documentation thins dramatically before 1500
  • There are three types of absence: (1) battles that weren't recorded at the time; (2) records that were lost or destroyed; (3) records written in non-European languages that weren't digitized into Wikidata
  • The ~90% rule: scholars estimate that the vast majority of battles fought before 1800 leave no surviving record whatsoever
  • CDB90 cutoff: the CBD90 dataset only goes to 1982, deliberately stopping before the chaotic post-Cold War era. FOr more recent wars, we must rely on Wikidata.

Section 04 — The Bias

One continent. Half the battles.

Of the 660 battles in CDB90, 51% took place in Western Europe. This is not because Europe was the world's most violent place — it's because Europe was the world's most documented one.

Drawing chart…
Key Takeaways
  • The 51% Western Europe figure is even more striking given that Europe is ~7% of Earth's land area and held a small fraction of global population through most of history
  • CDB90 was compiled by American social scientists working largely from English-language sources and the Western military history canon
  • The 14% Middle East figure reflects the Arab-Israeli wars, which were extensively studied by both U.S. and Israeli analysts
  • The entire continent of Africa accounts for only a handful of entries; Latin America barely appears; pre-colonial Asia is essentially invisible
  • You may notice a large proportion of battles with unknown region. This is not necessarily due to battle locations not being widely known, but due to poor quality of existing annotations in Wikidata. Sometimes Wikidata has battles with unknown location even though a simple Google search would find the location. Because of the limited time scope of this assignment, we decided to simply visualize this incomplete data instead of completing it. This, however, uncovers another source of bias — that of which battles Wikidata editors felt warranted more comprehensive annotation for easy querying.
Section 05 — Nation Portraits

Who fought, who attacked, who survived

Each bubble is a nation. Size reflects battles fought. Position shows win rate vs. how often they attacked. The upper-right corner — high attack, high win rate — is where empires live.

Key Takeaways
  • Germany (262 battles, 29.4% wins) is the starkest data point: almost entirely a defensive actor in WWII, losing battle after battle against overwhelming Allied numbers.
  • Israel punches above its weight: 72.9% win rate across 70 battles — the most tactically aggressive and successful small-force nation in the set.
  • Ottoman Empire appears only 23 times and wins just 8.7% — almost always as a defender, often outgunned. Its absence in earlier centuries (when it was dominant) is itself a data bias story.

A nation's win rate in battle tells you less about its soldiers than about which wars its historians chose to count.

Section 06 — Great Rivalries

The pairings that defined modern warfare

Germany vs. the United States alone accounts for 136 recorded battles — more than the entire U.S. Civil War. These are the recurring matchups that the CDB90 dataset was, in many ways, built to understand.

Key Takeaways
  • The Germany–USA dyad (136 battles) is almost entirely the Western Front of WWII: France, Italy, the Ardennes, the Rhine crossings.
  • Confederacy–USA (49) is one war, one theater, four years — a reminder that civil wars generate intense, concentrated battle records.
  • Notice what rivalries are absent: Britain–France across the Hundred Years' War, Mongols vs. anyone, any African conflict.
Section 07 — The Cost

The grinding arithmetic of casualties

Casualty rate: the fraction of a force destroyed in a single engagement. In 1973, the Yom Kippur War achieved rates under 2%. In 1916, battles consumed forces faster than they could be replaced.

Each dot below is a single battle. The vertical axis shows what percentage of the combined force was killed or wounded, using total casualties over the course of the fight divided by the strength initially committed on both sides. Because reinforcements and replacement drafts are fed into long battles, that ratio can occasionally exceed 100%. The horizontal axis is time; WWI sits at the apex of a catastrophic peak.

Key Takeaways
  • The First Somme (1916): 1,170,000 casualties from ~900,000 committed troops — a rate above 100% because replacement drafts were fed into the battle continuously.
  • Ypres III (Third Ypres / Passchendaele, 1917): similar story — 670,000 casualties, 116% of committed strength.
  • The Yom Kippur War cluster (1973) sits near the bottom — modern electronic warfare and precision anti-tank missiles dramatically reduced casualty rates.
Section 08 — The Shape of Battle

How the battlefield expanded — then contracted

Front width measures how broad a battle's contact zone was. From dueling lines of a few kilometers in the 17th century, through the continent-spanning fronts of WWI, and back down to the precise corridors of modern armored warfare.

Key Takeaways
  • 17th–18th century battles: front widths typically 1–5 km — constrained by the effective range of muskets and the need to maintain close-order formation.
  • WWI explosion: fronts of 64–200+ km. These were not single battles but coordinated attacks along industrial-scale defensive lines.
  • 1973 contraction: Israeli and Egyptian forces operated on fronts of 10–50 km — precision anti-tank missiles made wider dispersion dangerous.
Section 09 — The Mechanics of Victory

What actually determines who wins

The CDB90 dataset was built to answer one question: what factors determine victory? Each of 660 battles is scored across military variables. The answers challenge popular assumptions about why armies win.

Loading battle outcomes…
Key Guidelines
  • Surprise and superior leadership show the highest win-rate lift — tactical initiative beats raw material superiority.
  • Technology advantage (techa) has a surprisingly modest effect on its own: having better weapons doesn't guarantee victory without logistics and leadership.
  • The attacker wins ~88% of battles regardless of terrain or force ratio — this is partly a definition artifact, but it's still a striking asymmetry.
  • What exactly does superior leadership mean? Such factors encourage subjective, post-hoc interpretations that help justify the decisions of whoever won.

Bias doesn't just show in what battles are documented — it shows in the depth to which they are documented.

As you move through Sections 10–16, you may notice that the number of battles represented drops significantly from the overviews above. The terrain of a battle, the weather, the quality of commanders, the count of tanks and cavalry — this granular tactical information was only recorded for battles that the U.S. Army's Concepts Analysis Agency chose to study in depth, in the 1980s, for explicitly operational purposes.

That selection was itself a choice shaped by institutional priorities: Western military tradition, Cold War strategic anxieties, and English-language sources. The battles for which we have no terrain data, no weather codes, no commander records are not necessarily unimportant. They may simply be the ones no American analyst ever needed to win an argument about NATO doctrine.

A note on the numbers below
  • Terrain data (Section 10) covers all 660 CDB90 battles but only 3 terrain codes per battle — a simplification of real geography.
  • Weather records (Section 11) are similarly present for all 660 battles, though many battles list only one condition.
  • Mechanization data (Section 13) depends on belligerent-level records in belligerents.csv — unit counts are missing or zero for many early battles where such equipment did not exist.
  • Duration records (Section 14) come from archival timestamps and are only as precise as the original source allowed.
  • Commander data (Section 15) reflects only commanders named in CDB90 — many battles list only a national force, not an individual.
  • The "decisive" flag (Section 16) is an editorial judgment made by analysts four decades ago. Which battles deserved that label — and whose judgment it was — is itself a question worth sitting with.

Epilogue — The Silence

What the dataset cannot tell you

Every battle in these datasets was fought by people who bled and died. But the dataset only knows the ones someone thought worth remembering. The others left no echo.

Key Takeaways
  • Historians believe that only a small fraction of battles in human history have any surviving written record
  • The wars of the Songhai Empire, the Aztec flower wars, the battles of the Maurya Empire's expansion, the Mongol conquest of Central Asia - all of these are missing from the modern record.
  • The erasure of history through the destruction or neglect of non-Western records has a name - "archival violence"
  • Do the gaps in the data mirror broader gaps in how Western societies value the lives lost in non-Western conflicts

A dataset is not just a record of what happened. It is a record of what someone with resources, time, and a particular set of questions decided to write down.
