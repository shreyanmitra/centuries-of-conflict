Chapter I
The Geographic Shift of War

The amber circle marks the weighted centroid of documented conflict — the "center of gravity" of recorded fighting in each era. Its drift is real history, editorial silence, and the expanding reach of literacy, all at once.

01 — Before 500 AD
The Ancient World
Conflict in the ancient world was centered on the Mediterranean basin and Mesopotamia — the cradle of literate civilization. Persian, Greek, Roman, and Chinese sources preserve battle records, but vast regions of Africa, the Americas, and much of Asia go undocumented.

02 — 500 to 1399
The Medieval World
The Mongol conquests reshaped Eurasia and left a trail of documented engagements across Central Asia and China. Islamic expansion pushes the centroid east and south. Europe's crusades are well-documented; Sub-Saharan Africa and the Americas remain blank.
Centroid: computing…
03 — 1400 to 1699
The Age of Gunpowder
European military historians begin systematically recording battles. The European centroid pull becomes pronounced — not because Europe was uniquely violent, but because European scribes uniquely survived. The Ottoman and Mughal empires leave records; the rest of the world, largely silent.
Centroid: computing…
04 — 1700 to 1899
The Colonial Era
European powers project force globally, and their documentation does too. Colonial wars in India, Africa, and the Americas appear because European officers filed reports. Indigenous resistance goes unrecorded — the record still belongs to the colonizers.
Centroid: computing…
05 — 1900 to 1945
The World Wars
The two World Wars generated the densest documentation in history. Battles on the Eastern Front, North Africa, and the Pacific are meticulously recorded. The centroid snaps back toward Europe and East Asia — the dataset is richest here, and most Eurocentric.
Centroid: computing…
06 — 1946 to Present
The Contemporary Period
Post-1945 conflict genuinely globalizes. The Middle East, Southeast Asia, and sub-Saharan Africa appear in force for the first time. Wikipedia's global editorial community closes some gaps — the centroid's southeastward drift is partly real history, partly belated documentation.

A Deeper Bias
Even the "Gold Standard" is European

The CDB90 (U.S. Army Concepts Analysis Agency, 1984) is the most thoroughly coded land-battle database ever assembled — 660 battles, four centuries, tactical data for every engagement. Plot its battles on a world map, and the problem is immediately visible: roughly 82% fall in Europe or on European colonial frontiers. Not a failure of its compilers — a structural truth about which military records survive in forms usable by Western researchers.

CDB90 · 660 Battles · 1600–1973
The World's Most Detailed Battle Database Has Almost No Data on Most of the World
Of 660 battles in CDB90, ~82% are fought in Europe or on European colonial frontiers. Sub-Saharan Africa — which experienced constant conflict throughout this period — contributes fewer than 10 battles. Every attacker win-rate and tactical lesson drawn from CDB90 is primarily a lesson about how European-tradition armies fight.
